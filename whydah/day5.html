The Numbers

10 minutes · solo · quiet

Three numbers are on the board. They come from the records of our ship's 1716 voyage. The year before the pirates took her.

367 people forced aboard at Ouidah, West Africa
312 people still alive when she reached Jamaica
55 people who died at sea

Check the subtraction yourself. 367 minus 312. The math takes ten seconds. The meaning takes longer.

Every one of those numbers was a person. A name. A family. A language. A favorite food. Nobody on the ship wrote their names down. The ship's papers called them cargo. There is a word for treating a person like a thing: dehumanization.

Yesterday you drew Leg 2 and wrote PEOPLE on it. These are the people.

LOG IT: Copy the three numbers to the top of THE CROSSING page. Under them, one line: what is one thing a number cannot tell you about a person?

One Sentence of Testimony

~40 minutes with your teacher · read together, then write alone

In 1756, a boy in West Africa was kidnapped and forced onto a slave ship. He wrote later that he was about eleven years old. His name was Olaudah Equiano. He survived the crossing. Years later he bought his own freedom. Then he wrote his life down, so nobody could say it did not happen.

Here is one sentence from his book. We read one sentence today. One is enough.

"The shrieks of the women, and the groans of the dying, rendered the whole a scene of horror almost inconceivable."

Written by Olaudah Equiano about his own crossing in 1756, forty years after the Whydah.

Equiano was not on the Whydah. He crossed the same water forty years after her. We read him anyway, and here is why. The Whydah's 367 captives left no written words. His words stand in for theirs.

This happened to real people. We read it because looking away erases them.

SILENT WRITE: THE CROSSING page. Write anything true. What you feel counts. A question counts. One word counts. Nobody reads it unless you choose. If you need to stop writing and just sit, that is allowed. The room stays quiet until your teacher calls time.

If today sits heavy on you, that is the right response.

Put It on the Map

~15 minutes · solo, then all together · your Day 4 triangle, from your folder

A map tells the truth or it hides it. Yesterday you wrote PEOPLE on Leg 2. Today that leg gets its numbers.

  1. Take your Day 4 triangle out of your folder.
  2. Along Leg 2, write the route and the year: Ouidah to Jamaica, 1716.
  3. Under it, the count: 367 aboard. 312 survived. 55 died at sea.
  4. Add a small mark of respect at the middle of the leg. A star. A circle. You choose it.

Then we add the same leg to the class route map, together.

One more true thing before you file your map. After the pirates took this ship, about one sailor in four aboard her was a Black man. Many had freed themselves from this same trade. On the Whydah they sailed free. The records we have say they got an equal share. Their stories are on the People tab.
