The Crossing

~20 minutes · quiet block · your Day 4 triangle, from your folder

Yesterday you wrote PEOPLE on Leg 2. Today, before anything else, that leg gets the truth it is owed. Three numbers, from the records of our ship's 1716 voyage. The year before the pirates took her.

367 people forced aboard at Ouidah, West Africa
312 people still alive when she reached Jamaica
55 people who died at sea

Every number was a person. A name. A family. Nobody on the ship wrote their names down. The ship's papers called them cargo. A person is not cargo.

One survivor of a later crossing, Olaudah Equiano, grew up, bought his own freedom, and wrote his whole life down so nobody could say it did not happen. His story is on the People tab if you want more.

  1. Copy the three numbers to the top of THE CROSSING page.
  2. Take out your Day 4 triangle. On Leg 2 write: Ouidah to Jamaica, 1716 and the count.
  3. Add a small mark of respect at the middle of the leg. A star. A circle. You choose it.
LOG IT: Under your numbers, one line: what is one thing a number cannot tell you about a person?

This block stays quiet. No points here. Some things you do not race.

The Flip

~10 minutes with your teacher · listen up

Now the second story. February 1717. Bellamy's crew catches the Whydah near the Bahamas. The records we have say taking her cost no lives. And the ship flips.

Same planks. Same sails. A different deal. The hold that carried captives now carries a free crew. About one sailor in four aboard her was a Black man, and many had freed themselves from the same trade she was built for. On this ship they voted. They ate what the captain ate. The records we have say every man got an equal share.

The machine did not change. These men found a way out of it. That is the story you work on for the rest of today.

The Deal · Would You Sign?

~20 minutes · pairs, then corners · THE DEAL page

Why would a sailor join pirates? Look at the two deals on the table in 1717 and decide for yourself.

Honest sailor low pay, often held back for months. The captain's word is law. Flogging is legal. The navy can grab you off the street.
Pirate crew a vote for captain. Written rules the crew signs: the articles. An equal share. Payment if you lose a hand or a leg.

Honesty first: the Whydah's own articles never survived. We know the pirate deal from other crews, like Bartholomew Roberts' articles from 1721. And historians still argue how equal "equal" really was. Hold both.

  1. Draw the two columns on THE DEAL page. Fill each side: at least three things.
  2. Under them, one sentence: which deal would YOU take, and what would scare you about it?
  3. When your teacher calls it: walk to your corner. SIGN · STAY HONEST · NEED TO KNOW MORE.
LOG IT: Your columns and your sentence. Keep them. Monday the whole class votes for real, and this page is your evidence.

The Question · Circle Up

8 minutes · whole crew, one circle · chairs or standing

What would freedom have meant to someone who joined this ship?

We end today in a circle. One object goes around the ring. Whoever holds it may speak. Everyone else listens.

  • When it reaches you: answer the question, or say one word, or name the person you met on the People tab.
  • You may also pass. Say "pass" and hand it on. That counts as an answer.
  • No replying to what someone else said. The circle is for hearing, not debating.

Silence is a full answer.
