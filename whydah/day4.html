Adopt Your Ship

~12 minutes · with your first mate · one Chromebook per pair, but EACH of you adopts your own ship

Right now, this minute, thousands of real ships are crossing the ocean. You can see every one of them. Pick yours.

Draw the Triangle

~12 minutes · pairs · paper (map sheet if you got one, freehand is fine)

Ships in 1717 didn't sail wherever they wanted. Wind decided. Near the equator the trade winds blow west. Up north the westerlies blow east. Together they spin the Atlantic like a giant slow wheel called the gyre. Sailors rode it like a conveyor belt.

That wheel forced a route with three legs. On your TRIANGLE page, draw the Atlantic: Europe top right, Africa bottom right, the Americas on the left. Then draw and label the legs:

  1. Leg 1: London south to West Africa. Cargo: trade goods and money.
  2. Leg 2: West Africa west to the Caribbean. Cargo: enslaved people. The Whydah was built for this leg. She carried 367 human beings. She is named for the slave port of Ouidah.
  3. Leg 3: The Caribbean north and east, home to Europe. Cargo: sugar, silver, profit.
LOG IT: Label every leg with its cargo. All three. Leg 2's cargo was people. Write that word on your map. A map that skips it is lying by leaving out.

Yesterday's callback: whose eyes is YOUR triangle map drawn from? The merchant counting profit sees one map. A person chained below deck sees another. Hold that thought for tomorrow.

The Machine, Then and Now

~10 minutes · pairs

Now zoom out. The whole world's shipping, live.

Open the global live map →
  1. Where do ships bunch up today? Look at the Atlantic. Look at the narrow squeezes.
  2. Put your paper triangle next to the screen. Is the ocean still a machine with set routes? What changed? What didn't?
  3. Your adopted ship is part of this machine. Whose stuff is it carrying? Who gets paid?
LOG IT: One sentence, two halves: "In 1717, ______ benefited when a ship crossed the Atlantic. Today, ______ benefits." Be specific. "People" is too easy.

Fast finishers: sketch "Routes of My Life" on scrap paper. Five places that made you, connected by lines. It becomes raw material for a story map project. Or earn game time: Navigator awaits.
