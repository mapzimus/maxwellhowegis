One Map, 267 Years

~8 minutes · with your first mate · one Chromebook per pair

One mission fails. One mark on a map survives it. 267 years later, that mark finds a ship. Watch how.

April 26, 1717 our ship breaks apart in a storm off Cape Cod. The sea keeps her.
One week later mapmaker Cyprian Southack rows a shortcut called Jeremiah's Gutter to reach her. Too late. The beach is picked clean. He posts notices. Nobody returns a thing.
Before he sails home almost empty-handed, he does ONE thing right: on his chart, in his own hand, he marks where she went down.
1984 diver Barry Clifford reads that same 267-year-old mark and finds her. In 1985 his crew raises a bell: THE WHYDAH GALLY 1716. Proof.

Southack's mission was a failure. His mark was not. That is the whole lesson before you touch a grid: a location can outlive everything else about a story.

Open Southack's chart → The real finds →
  1. Find Southack's wreck note on the chart, near Wellfleet. He spelled the ship "Whido."
  2. Find his note about the dead. The town buried 102 men from our ship.
  3. Look at the shape of the Cape. He charted it from a small boat in rough weather. What would you double-check before trusting it?
LOG IT: Top of your FINDS LOG page, one line: the salvage failed, so what did Southack actually bring home that mattered?

Mark Your Dig

~10 minutes · solo, then crew huddle · chart on screen, grid on paper

The chart gets you close. Close is not found.

The wreck lies under sand off Marconi Beach, near 41.891 north, 69.957 west. Divers there do not wander around with shovels. They drop a grid on the seabed and give every square a name. Then they dig square by square.

Pretend your 6x6 grid is that patch of seabed. Time to bet.

  1. Study the chart one last time. Then look at your 36 squares.
  2. Pick the one square you would dig first. Gut call. Mark a small dot in it.
  3. Under the grid, one line: why that square?
  4. Crew huddle: compare dots. Agree on your crew's opening call for the game.
CALLED SHOT: when your crew's opening call gets played in THE DIG, if that square turns out to hold a real find, your whole crew banks 1 bonus point before the game even really starts. Gut calls count.
3

THE DIG

~25 minutes · whole room, crew vs crew · paper only, lids down

Your teacher holds the seabed: a fixed map of the 36 squares, kept on their own screen, nothing printed, nothing handed out. Eight squares hold something. The rest are sand. The squares don't move once the game starts, so a smart guess is a real bet, not a coin flip.

Down there somewhere: coins. A bell. A shoe. Concretions. A concretion is sand and rusted iron fused into one rock. Surprises hide inside.

  1. Crews call in turns. One square per turn. Letter first, like a sailor: "B4."
  2. Your teacher calls back what is there. Every crew marks every call on their own grid. X for sand. A word for a find.
  3. A dug square stays dug. Call it again and your turn is gone. The grid remembers. So should you.
  4. A find counts only when it is in your FINDS LOG: square, find, one line on what it might tell us. No square, no find.
  5. Watch where the hits land. Wrecks leave patterns. Crews that read the pattern win.

Teacher note: nothing to print. The answer grid lives in runsheets.html (d12) on your own device — same drive, no handout, never shown to the room.

LOG IT: FINDS LOG, one line per find: SQUARE · FIND · what it might tell us. Write it so a stranger could redo your dig.
THE DIG: first crew to 3 logged finds: 5 points. Second crew: 2. The bell is special. It names the ship. Raise it for 1 bonus point.

When the seabed empties, your teacher reveals the answer grid. Look at the shape the finds make. Wrecks do not scatter randomly. Crew done logging while others dig? Sharpen your tell-us lines. Best one in the room wins points. Then Navigator awaits.

The Question

2 minutes · think, then write

Why does evidence matter for the story you tell?

A shoe in a glass case is just sad. A shoe logged at one square of one wreck became a real boy named John King. Location turned a thing into a story.

LOG IT: One sentence at the bottom of your FINDS LOG: why does WHERE you found it matter as much as WHAT you found?

Captain's Log

5 minutes · solo · in your captain's voice

Three hundred years from now, divers drop a grid on YOUR ship's wreck site. They raise one object.

LOG IT: What is the object? What true thing does it prove about your ship? What would it get wrong? Two or three sentences, captain's voice. Date it like a sailor: "27 July 2026."
