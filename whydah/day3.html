Day 3 · Week 1 — Setting Sail

Reading the 1719 World Map

Drawn in London — from the voyages of buccaneers and privateers. Time to interrogate it.
0

Rig your Analysis Card

2 minutes · solo · paper + pencil

One sheet, folded like a book. Cover: your name, "Day 3," the date. Inside, write these four field labels — leave room under each:

THE MAPMAKER — who drew it, and whose travels did he copy?
WHAT IT SHOWS / COULDN'T SHOW — at least 2 of each
WHOSE EYES? — the world drawn from whose point of view?
THE ARGUMENT — one claim this map is making

Three of these — the mapmaker, whose eyes, and the argument — are your exit ticket today. This is a real historian's tool: you're not just looking at the map, you're cross-examining it.

1

Something's Wrong

~10 minutes · with your first mate (pairs)

This is Herman Moll's "New & Correct Map of the Whole World," 1719 — as new and correct as maps got. Except…

Open the FULL map →

Zoom: Ctrl + scroll (or pinch). Bonus: the library mega-scan — huge, can be slow.

Detective hunt — find what's wrong to modern eyes:

  1. Look at California, very closely. What did Moll do to it?
  2. Find the words "PARTS UNKNOWN." Now find them again, somewhere else. How many can you spot?
  3. Bottom-right: find the continent that just… stops, like the artist put the pen down.
  4. What does Moll call the Pacific Ocean? (Hint: it's huge, bottom-left.)
LOG IT: On your card — THE MAPMAKER (his name and year are in the fancy title box, top-left) + two things under WHAT IT SHOWS / COULDN'T SHOW. (California-as-an-island and the missing coast are things Moll couldn't show — nobody had sailed there yet.)

Sharp-eyes bonus: find the round inset map of the North Pole — a big circle labeled "THE NORTH POLE," bottom-left, below the tip of South America. Why would Moll draw the pole separately instead of on the main map? (Day-2 clue: what does Mercator do to the top and bottom of the world?)

Big idea: these aren't just goofs. They're what people in 1719 believed — and what they wanted to believe. Hold that for Mission 3.

2

Whose Voyages?

~10 minutes · pairs

Moll almost never crossed an ocean himself. So how did he draw far-off coasts? He built his maps from the voyages of people who HAD been there — sailors, explorers, buccaneers and privateers. (A privateer = a pirate with a government license.) Their hard-won knowledge of coasts and currents had a name: intelligence.

  1. Read the fancy title box (top-left). It brags about "the most Remarkable Tracks" — find a voyage track drawn across an ocean. (One even names a captain and a year.)
  2. The oceans are full of tiny arrows, with labels like "Trade Winds." What do they show — and why would a sailor pay for that? (Day-2 clue: what powered every ship in 1719?)
  3. Day-2 callback: this map is on Mercator. So a straight track across the sea is a — what? (You learned the word yesterday.)
LOG IT: Add to THE MAPMAKER whose travels Moll copied. Two names right on this map: William Dampier — a buccaneer who sailed around the world three times and charted every coast (Moll engraved Dampier's own maps) · Woodes Rogers — a privateer, later a pirate-hunter, whose voyage is tracked across the Pacific here.
3

The Argument

~12 minutes · pairs · the core

Yesterday you proved every map chooses. Today's map isn't just choosing — it's arguing. Find the argument.

  1. Hunt for names starting with "New" — find at least two. Who's doing the naming? Who already lived there?
  2. "PARTS UNKNOWN" doesn't mean empty. Unknown to whom?
  3. Study the title-box artwork (top-left): who's sitting comfortably? Who's standing, working, kneeling? Art like this is never an accident.
LOG IT: Fill WHOSE EYES? (drawn from whose point of view — the people doing the naming, or the people already living there?) and THE ARGUMENT — one sentence: "This map argues that…"

On your feet: when Mr. Howe calls for it, walk up and write your one sentence on the board. Every crew's claim goes up.

Talk with your first mate: who would pay for a map like this in 1719 — and what did they want it to say?

4

The Whydah's World

~6 minutes · pairs · the payoff

Moll drew this two years after our ship went down. Her whole world is on it — if you know where to look. Find the three places:

  1. GUINEA — the West African coast she was built to sail to, to buy enslaved people. (She's named for a port there: Ouidah.)
  2. The Caribbean — where she delivered them, and where Bellamy's pirates later captured her.
  3. New England — not a stop on the slave route. It's only where the pirates sailed her afterward, and where she wrecked and still lies (yesterday's live-cam water).
LOG IT: One sentence on the back of your card: this map shows the Whydah's whole world — but what about her story can a map like this never show?

Quiet Water: the reflection

~5 minutes · solo · the whole-class closer — wait for Mr. Howe to call it, then lids down

On the back of your Analysis Card, write (or draw) your answer:

"If the 1719 map is an argument —
what argument is it making, and who is making it?"

Moll, who drew it? The pirates, who fed him the information? The empire, who bought it? There's a real case for each. Make yours.
