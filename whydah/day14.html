One Harbor, Two Centuries

The Record and the Rescue

~8 minutes · pairs · a quiet mission, no race on this one

Salem got rich on the water. Cod and lumber sailed out to the Caribbean plantations. Sugar, molasses, and rum sailed back. Those plantations ran on the labor of enslaved people. That trade built the wharves you walked.

And the harder page of the ledger: after the Revolution, some Salem merchants sent slave ships of their own. The record survives. Named ships. Named owners. A historian who read those old papers called Salem the most active slave-trade port in Massachusetts in those years. That is our town's record. We read it straight, like historians.

Same waters, one more story. Philip Ashton, a young fisherman from Marblehead, the next town over. June 1722: pirates seize his fishing boat off Nova Scotia. Not Bellamy. A crueler captain named Ned Low. Ashton refuses to turn pirate. They hold him anyway. Months later he slips away on an island off Honduras and survives there almost two years, alone. In 1725 a ship out of Salem finds him and carries him home. His parents thought he was dead. He wrote the whole story down. You can still read it.

The Salem tab: the record + Ashton →
  1. Find one named Salem ship from the slave-trade record. Write its name.
  2. Find one Ashton fact this page did not give you. Write it.
LOG IT: Two short lines on MY ITEM'S VOYAGE: "The record says ___." and "Ashton ___."

Trace One Item

~40 minutes · solo · paper, a label, and the world map sheet

The machine from 1717 never shut down. It got bigger, faster, and harder to see. Proof? You are wearing some of its cargo right now.

  1. Pick one thing you own or wear today. Shoe, hoodie, backpack, water bottle, pencil case.
  2. Find the label. "Made in ___." Check the collar, the waistband, the shoe tongue, the bottle base. No label? Pick another item.
  3. Map it. On the world sheet: a dot where it was made. A dot on Salem. Now draw its probable route by sea. Almost everything heavy crosses by ship, not by plane.
  4. Name the ship. For almost everything you own: a container ship. Steel boxes, thousands of them per ship.
  5. Name the squeeze points. Yesterday's list: Suez, Malacca, Panama, the Red Sea. Made in East Asia? It likely rode through Malacca, then Suez, or across the Pacific and through Panama. Made in Europe? Straight across the Atlantic. Not sure? Check the live map and follow the traffic.

Now hold your item and think about your triangle map from Day 4. Your object rode the same machine the Whydah did. Different engine. Same machine: set routes, narrow squeezes, somebody profits, somebody works out of sight.

LABEL HUNT: first crew to log items made on three different continents wins 5 league points. Real labels only. Shout it when you have three.
LOG IT: MY ITEM'S VOYAGE page: item · where it was made · ship type · squeeze points. Route drawn on the map sheet. Then the sentence that matters, two halves: "In 1717, ___. Today, ___." That sentence is your exit ticket.

Fast finishers: trace a second item from a different continent. Or ask the harder question: who touched your item between the factory and you? List every pair of hands you can imagine.

The Question

On your way out · say it to your first mate

What's still happening today that started in 1717?

You drew part of the answer on your map sheet. Say it in one sentence at the door.

wharfcontainerimportglobal trade

wharf = a platform built from shore into the harbor so ships can load and unload · container = the standard steel box that moves the world's cargo, thousands to a ship · import = goods carried into a country to be sold there · global trade = the system that moves goods between countries across the world's oceans
