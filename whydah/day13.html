The Squeeze Hunt

~12 minutes · with your first mate · one Chromebook per pair

Zoom out on the live map and the ocean looks free. It is not. Ships crowd into a few narrow doors, and they line up to get through. Find the crowds.

Open the global live map →

Four doors to hunt. Jump straight to any of them:

  1. Suez Canal. One canal through a desert. About 12 percent of world trade squeezes through it.
  2. Strait of Malacca. About a third of global shipping rides this one strait.
  3. Red Sea, Bab-el-Mandeb. The narrow south door of the Red Sea. Since 2023, attacks on cargo ships here have pushed many crews to sail the long way around Africa instead. Thousands of miles longer.
  4. Panama Canal. A shortcut cut through a continent. In 2023 and 2024 a drought cut its daily traffic by about a third. Rain refilled it. Sailors still watch the sky there.
LOG IT: For each door you visit, write on THE SQUEEZE page: the chokepoint's name. What makes the squeeze: land on both sides? a canal? a strait? And one real ship you can see waiting in the line.
THE SQUEEZE HUNT: log two doors and shout "SQUEEZE." First crew: 5 points. Second: 2. Log all four before missions end: 3 bonus points.

Check your adopted ship. Is a door ahead on its route? Your captain will want that in tonight's log.

2

Plot Like It's 1717

~18 minutes · solo · DEAD RECKONING sheet + pencil · lids at 45

It is spring 1717. You are the navigator. Your sloop lies off Jamaica, and the plan is the same one Bellamy sailed: run north for the Carolinas. He took more than 50 ships in about a year on this hunting ground.

You have no GPS. You have three numbers: heading, speed, time. That is dead reckoning. Plot it:

  1. Mark your start dot near the bottom of the grid. That is Jamaica.
  2. Leg 1: heading 045°, speed 5 knots, 6 hours. Distance = speed times time = 30 nautical miles. One square = 10, so draw 3 squares toward 045°. Use the compass rose.
  3. Leg 2: heading 030°, 4 knots, 8 hours. You do the math this time. Draw it from where Leg 1 ended.
  4. Leg 3: heading 010°, 6 knots, 5 hours. Same drill.
  5. The end of Leg 3 is where you think you are. Mark it with an X.

Now the sea cheats. A current pushed you 10 nautical miles west and you never felt it. Mark a dot one full square west of your X. That dot is where you really are. The gap between X and dot is dead reckoning error. It grew every day at sea.

So how did pirates ever find prey on all that water? Mostly they did not chase. They parked at the doors. Even a lost ship must pass a door. Your Leg 1 runs at one: the Windward Passage, the gap between Cuba and Hispaniola. Bellamy took the Whydah herself near it, after a three-day chase.

LOG IT: You're done when your sheet shows three legs drawn, three distances written, the X, the dot, and the gap labeled ERROR.
3

The Same Machine

~10 minutes · talk with your first mate, write solo · THE SQUEEZE page

1717 and 2026. Three hundred years apart, and piracy runs on the same machine. Three parts:

1. TRAFFIC WORTH ROBBING rich cargo, moving on a schedule
2. GEOGRAPHY THAT SQUEEZES IT straits, canals, island gaps. The ocean narrows to a door and the ships line up.
3. WEAK POLICING nobody guarding that door

All three lined up in the 1717 Caribbean. Treasure and trade ships on set routes. Island gaps like the Windward Passage. And navies an ocean away.

Do they line up today? Test it. Not long ago, pirates seized a cargo ship called the Ruen off Somalia. In 2024, navy sailors freed the ship and its crew. Ask the three questions: was the cargo worth money? Did geography squeeze the ships into a line? Was anyone guarding the water?

LOG IT: On THE SQUEEZE page, make a small table. Three rows: the three factors. Two columns: Windward Passage 1717 and one modern chokepoint you logged. Fill all six boxes. Your modern column must name a real place.

Modern piracy is real, and it is about ships and cargo. Crews train for it. Navies patrol for it. It is a geography problem, and you now know the three parts. That is why it belongs on your map.
