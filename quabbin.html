Quabbin: A Valley Chosen, Four Towns Erased

Self-directed GIS Study · 2026 · Open Data · Fully Reproducible

In 1938, Massachusetts did something drastic: it disincorporated four entire towns — Dana, Enfield, Greenwich, and Prescott — and flooded the Swift River Valley to build the Quabbin Reservoir. About 2,500 people lost their homes so that Boston, 65 miles east, could have water.

I wanted to tell that story as a multi-layer GIS study — where several spatial layers, read together, converge on a single narrative — so I built the whole thing in R: terrain, reservoir, watershed, modern town boundaries, and population displacement, each from open public data.

Unlike my earlier coursework, this one ships its code. A single Rscript run_all.R pulls a DEM from AWS Terrain Tiles, carves the reservoir out of its 530-foot full-pool surface, fetches USGS watershed units and US Census boundaries, and renders the ten figures below — then animates the valley filling frame by frame and exports the layers behind a live interactive map — reproducibly, with every download cached and every network call degrading gracefully if a service is down.

The layers agree: the valley's geography made it an obvious basin to flood, and the four towns — already shrinking for decades — were erased from the map, their land parcelled out to the towns that still ring the water today.

Going deeper, I added the dimensions a static map can't show: the towns' real decennial census counts (every one already shrinking before 1920), a flood-fill animation raising the water from the valley floor to the 1946 full pool, the aqueduct that carries the water ~100 km east to the Boston metro — the city the valley was drowned for — and an interactive web map where you raise the water yourself.

Watch the valley drown

The reservoir didn't appear overnight — once the dam was sealed in 1939, the Swift River backed up for seven years, reaching its 530-foot full pool in 1946. The animation below raises the water stage by stage up the real terrain; in the interactive map you can drag the slider yourself, read each town's census history, and follow the aqueduct east to Boston.
